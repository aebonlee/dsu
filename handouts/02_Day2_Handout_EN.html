Periods 1–2 · Assessment Types + Essay Items & Model Answers

Period 1 · The Five Faces of Assessment in the Humanities & Arts

Period 160 minLevel FoundationalPriority ★★
Learning goal Distinguish the characteristics and best uses of essay, critical-thinking, performance, portfolio, and presentation assessments. (Understanding)
Key content
  • ① Essay: explaining what you know in writing — "Explain the meaning of ○○ using two supporting reasons."
  • ② Critical: evaluating a claim or a work against evidence — "Identify the weaknesses in this argument and refute them."
  • ③ Performance: showing what you can do with your body and hands — recitals, demonstrations, studio production (the heart of the arts)
  • ④ Portfolio: a collection that traces a semester of growth — process-centered assessment
  • ⑤ Presentation: conveying your thinking out loud — content + delivery + Q&A
  • Assessment types and the role of AI: design items and criteria together with AI, but observing performance and making the final judgment stays with the professor
  • 📦 Output: A combination chart of assessment types for my own course
Practice — paste straight into Claude
Practice 1Drawing the assessment blueprint for my course
[Role] You are an expert with 10 years of experience in university assessment design.
[Context] My course is [course name] (undergraduate [year level], [number of students] students), and the current assessment is [current method], which I feel does not really capture what the course is about.
[Request] Among the five types — essay, critical, performance, portfolio, and presentation — recommend the combination that best fits this course.
[Format] Present as a table: (1) the recommended combination and its weighting (2) the competency each assessment measures (3) the preparation work AI can help with for each type.
[Example] A sample combination: "Critical essay 40% + Portfolio 40% + Presentation 20%".
[Condition] Also show the grading burden (time required) so that the combination stays realistic.
Practice 2Experimenting with a change of type
[Role] You are an education consultant who has worked with many assessment-innovation cases.
[Context] Right now the final assessment in my course is [current method]. In the age of AI, I feel that simply checking memorization is not enough.
[Request] Design what this assessment would look like if I changed it to a combination of critical essay and portfolio.
[Format] A "current → proposed" comparison table + 3 advantages + 3 points to watch out for.
[Example] A sample caution: "A portfolio increases grading time — spread it out with two interim check-ins."
[Condition] Include practical problems such as student pushback or grading burden, along with ways to address them.
Practice 3Building performance and presentation criteria (arts & physical education)
[Role] You are an expert in designing performance assessments for the arts and physical education.
[Context] I need to assess [name of the performance/presentation task], and I want the criteria to be transparent so that no one misunderstands it as a subjective judgment.
[Request] Create the assessment criteria. For a performance, use three axes — technical accuracy / interpretation and expression / attitude; for a presentation, use three axes — content / delivery / Q&A.
[Format] A table dividing each axis into three levels + an observation checklist the professor can use on the day of the assessment.
[Example] Sample observation items: "☐ Maintains tempo in the opening ☐ Recovers gracefully after a mistake".
[Condition] Assume that observing and judging performance is the professor's responsibility — AI stops at designing the criteria.
Periods 1–2 · Assessment Types + Essay Items & Model Answers

Period 2 · Hands-On: Essay Items + Model Answers by Level

Period 260 minLevel IntermediatePriority ★★★
Learning goal Generate essay items for your own course along with model answers at each level. (Applying & Creating)
Key content
  • What makes a good essay item: what (the subject) + how (the thinking verb) + conditions (length, number of sources) must all be clear
  • Choosing the thinking verb: summarize (understanding) < compare (analysis) < evaluate (evaluation) < propose (creation)
  • "Model answers by level" means: writing weak, adequate, and strong answers in advance so you can see your grading criteria with your own eyes
  • Why three model answers are useful: they secure grading consistency and give students concrete examples of each level
  • Security principle, revisited: replace real exam items with similar practice items while working in class
  • 📦 Output: 2 essay items + a set of model answers at 3 levels
Practice — paste straight into Claude
Practice 1Designing essay items
[Role] You are an expert with 10 years of experience writing assessment items in [field of study].
[Context] I am preparing an essay assessment that checks the thinking skills of undergraduate [year level] students within the scope of [topic]. (These are practice items, not the real exam items.)
[Request] Create 2 essay items — one analytical ("compare and analyze") and one critical ("evaluate and refute").
[Format] For each item, state the required answer length and number of supporting reasons + a one-line note for the professor on the intent behind the item.
[Example] A sample item format: "Compare and analyze the difference in perspective between the two historical sources (600 characters, at least 2 supporting reasons)."
[Condition] Make sure "what + how (thinking verb) + conditions" all appear within the sentence itself.
Practice 2Generating model answers at 3 levels
[Role] You are an assessment expert who makes grading criteria visible through sample answers.
[Context] I want to stay consistent when grading item 1 that we just created, and I would also like to show students examples of each level.
[Request] Write the model answer for item 1 at three levels: (1) strong (full marks) (2) adequate (partial credit) (3) weak.
[Format] Under each answer, add 2 lines from a grader's point of view explaining "why this answer sits at this level".
[Example] A sample explanation: "Adequate: grasps the claim, but offers only one supporting reason and shows no awareness of counterarguments."
[Condition] Keep the three answers similar in length — the level should be separated by the quality of thinking, not by word count.
Practice 3Experimenting with difficulty adjustment
[Role] You are an assessment designer who fine-tunes item difficulty with precision.
[Context] I like item 1, and I would like to adapt it for classes at other year levels as well.
[Request] Adapt item 1 into (1) a version one step easier (for first-year students) and (2) a version one step harder (advanced, for third- and fourth-year students).
[Format] A three-column table: "Version / Item / What was changed (thinking verb, conditions, source material)".
[Example] A sample principle of adjustment: "Easier version — change 'compare' to 'identify two differences' and provide the source material."
[Condition] Explain the principle behind why the difficulty changed, so that I can apply it to other items on my own.
Periods 3–4 · Competency Rubrics + Scalable Feedback + Source Verification

Period 3 · Building a Rubric That Measures Analysis, Critique, and Creativity

Period 360 minLevel AdvancedPriority ★★★
Learning goal Write a competency-based grading rubric and validate it against your model answers. (Creating & Evaluating)
Key content
  • A competency rubric = a criteria table that measures not knowledge, but "the quality of thinking"
  • Breaking down the three core competencies: analysis (grasping structure, finding relationships) / critique (evaluating evidence, counterarguments) / creativity (making new connections, proposing alternatives)
  • How to describe levels: instead of vague words like "does well," describe behavior, as in "constructs a counterargument using at least two supporting reasons"
  • How to validate a rubric: grade the three model answers from period 2 with the rubric and check whether the scores come out as expected
  • Applying it in the arts: a performance rubric (technical accuracy / interpretation and expression / creativity) is built the same way
  • 📦 Output: 1 competency rubric (validated)
Practice — paste straight into Claude
Practice 1Generating a competency rubric
[Role] You are an expert with 10 years of experience designing educational assessment rubrics.
[Context] I need to grade [name of the assignment/item]. I want to measure the quality of thinking (analysis, critique, creativity) fairly, rather than memorized knowledge.
[Request] Create a grading rubric.
[Format] A table of 4 criteria (analytical skill / critical thinking / creativity / expression) × 4 levels (weak, 1 point, through outstanding, 4 points).
[Example] Sample wording for the outstanding cell: "Constructs a counterargument using at least two supporting reasons, and even acknowledges the limits of their own counterargument."
[Condition] No vague wording such as "does well" in any cell — describe it so that the student's concrete behavior is visible. Below the table, add 3 common mistakes made during grading.
Practice 2Validating the rubric with model answers
[Role] You are a quality reviewer who tests whether a rubric actually works.
[Context] I want to check whether the rubric we just created really separates the levels.
[Request] Using this rubric, grade each of the three model answers we made earlier (strong / adequate / weak).
[Format] A table with "Criterion / Strong answer: score and reasoning / Adequate answer: score and reasoning / Weak answer: score and reasoning" + a comparison of total scores.
[Example] A sample verdict: "Total scores — strong 14 / adequate 9 / weak 5 — the levels separate successfully."
[Condition] If there is a criterion where the total scores do not spread apart well, revise the level descriptions for that criterion and show me the result again.
Practice 3Creating a student-facing version of the rubric
[Role] You are a communication expert who translates assessment criteria into students' own language.
[Context] I plan to share the validated rubric with students along with the assignment instructions. I want publishing the criteria to double as guidance for learning.
[Request] Turn this rubric into a version meant for students.
[Format] Rewrite the top level of each criterion around examples, in the spirit of "an answer that does this is a good answer" + a 5-question self-check list to use before submitting.
[Example] A sample check item: "☐ Did I write even one sentence about what someone who disagrees with my claim might say?"
[Condition] Use the words students actually use rather than assessment jargon, and keep it within one A4 page.
Periods 3–4 · Competency Rubrics + Scalable Feedback + Source Verification

Period 4 · From One Feedback to All of Them + Verifying Evidence, Sources, and Fake Citations

Period 460 minLevel IntermediatePriority ★★★
Learning goal Turn a sample feedback into a template you can scale, and verify evidence and sources through web search. (Applying & Evaluating)
Key content
  • A strategy for personalized feedback: write one carefully crafted sample → extract its structure as a template → apply it to each student by changing only the individual notes
  • The three-part structure of feedback: what went well (with a specific quotation) → where to grow (including the method) → encouragement toward the next step
  • Privacy principle, revisited: remove student names and ID numbers, and enter only the minimum necessary from the assignment content
  • Catching fake citations (hallucinated references): always confirm whether a reference produced by AI actually exists — search the title, author, and year
  • Gathering evidence through web search: collect recent cases and statistics for your items and feedback via web search, and record the source along with them
  • A 5-step verification routine: ① tag every citation as "unverified" → ② search the title exactly as written (Google Scholar, RISS, DBpia) → ③ check against the original text → ④ delete anything you cannot find → ⑤ cite only what you have confirmed
  • Teach it to students too: add "when using AI, indicate whether the original text of every citation was checked" to your assignment requirements
  • 📦 Output: A feedback template + a source verification checklist
Practice — paste straight into Claude
Practice 1From a sample feedback to a scalable template
[Role] You are an education coach who analyzes the structure of good feedback.
[Context] Below is feedback I wrote carefully for the essay of student A (anonymized). I would like to extend this same quality to all 30 students.
[Request] Analyze the structure of this feedback and turn it into a template I can use for other students as well.
[Format] The template (leave the parts that change per student as [blanks], and keep the fixed frame as is) + 3 lines on how to use it.
[Example] A sample template sentence: "Your [strength] stands out in [specific quotation]. Next time, try [method]."
[Condition] Preserve my tone of voice, and leave the closing encouragement slot empty, marked "write this yourself".

Practice 2Training to hunt down fake citations
[Role] You are an academic librarian who verifies whether references really exist.
[Context] Some material generated by AI came with a reference list attached. Because fake citations are fatal in humanities and social science writing, I want to check them one by one.
[Request] Search the web and confirm whether the following references actually exist: [reference list]
[Format] A table with "Reference / Exists? / Link to the source consulted / Accuracy of author, year, and title".
[Example] Sample verdicts: "○○ (2021) — no search results, deletion recommended / △△ (2019) — exists, but the correct year is 2018."
[Condition] Mark any reference you cannot confirm as "deletion recommended", and remind me that the final judgment requires me to open the original text myself.
Practice 3Collecting and verifying evidence through web search
[Role] You are a meticulous academic researcher who always leaves the source behind.
[Context] I need recent supporting material (cases, statistics, research) for the essay assessments and feedback in [course name]. I cannot use anything whose source is unclear.
[Request] Search the web and find 3 pieces of supporting material on [topic] published within the last 2 years.
[Format] For each item, present in a table: (1) a one-line summary (2) the source (institution, publication date, link) (3) how to use it in assessment or feedback (4) the items I should verify myself in the original text.
[Example] A sample use: "As the source material for an essay item — have students interpret this statistic and build an argument from it."
[Condition] Exclude entirely any material whose source cannot be confirmed, and if you find fewer than 3, tell me how you changed the search terms and tried again.
Practice 4Varying feedback by student type
[Role] You are a professor with a counselor's instincts, attentive even to a student's state of mind.
[Context] The same feedback template has to land differently depending on where a student is.
[Request] Adapt my feedback template for three student types: (1) a student who does well but has grown complacent — offer a challenge (2) a student who works hard but does not know how — give concrete methods (3) a student who has lost confidence — help them rediscover their strengths.
[Format] One sample feedback for each type (within 5 lines).
[Example] A sample sentence for the rediscovering-strengths type: "The metaphor in this paragraph was the freshest in the class — try bringing that instinct into your main argument as well."
[Condition] Assume the same assignment situation for all three so that the differences stand out clearly.
